Why It Matters

Tracking European insider disclosures is harder than it looks

Under MAR Article 19, company directors and executives across the EU (and the UK post-Brexit under its own rules) must publicly disclose share transactions within 3 business days. The data is public — but it is scattered across more than a dozen national regulators, published in different formats, and rarely aggregated in one place.

🗂️

12+ regulatory sources

Each EU member state maintains its own disclosure database. BaFin for Germany, AMF for France, AFM for the Netherlands, FSMA for Belgium — none of them use the same format.

⚡

3-day disclosure window

European insiders have up to 3 business days to report a transaction. Acting quickly on fresh data matters — stale aggregators can lag by days or weeks.

🔍

Signal extraction is non-trivial

Raw regulatory filings contain noise: option exercises, inheritance, DRIP purchases. Meaningful signal requires filtering and classifying each transaction type.

📈

Proven alpha source

Academic research (Jeng, Metrick & Zeckhauser 2003) shows insider purchase portfolios generate significant abnormal returns. European data has historically been under-covered by retail tools.

What is the difference between US and European insider trading rules?

US insiders file Form 4 with the SEC within 2 business days of a transaction, and the data flows into a single centralised EDGAR database. European insiders disclose under MAR Article 19 within 3 business days to their national regulator — but the data is spread across 12+ separate regulatory systems, each with its own format. That fragmentation is the core problem that aggregators like InsidersAlpha solve.
